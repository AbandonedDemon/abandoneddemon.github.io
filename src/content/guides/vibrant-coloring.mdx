<GuideStep id="pass-1" num="01" kicker="Foundation · ~10 min · The non-pass pass" title="Before you touch a slider.">
  <Goal>Establish the document, identify what's already in the image, and commit to a 3–5 color target palette before any layer goes down.</Goal>

  <StepsList>
    <li><strong>Mode &amp; color space.</strong> Image → Mode → <code>16 Bits/Channel</code> for safety on heavy grades. <em>Bit depth = how many color values per pixel; 16-bit gives you 256× more values than 8-bit, which prevents banding when you stack heavy adjustments.</em> Then Edit → Color Settings → Working RGB <code>sRGB IEC61966-2.1</code> — the long-named option is the right one for anything posted online.</li>
    <li><strong>Convert source to Smart Object.</strong> Right-click the layer → <code>Convert to Smart Object</code>. Lets you re-edit Camera Raw in Pass 05 without baking anything in.</li>
    <li><strong>Read the source.</strong> Eyedropper across the image. What hues already exist? What's the dominant temperature? What's the brightest point, the darkest? Five seconds of looking saves an hour of fixing.</li>
    <li><strong>Pick a target palette.</strong> 3–5 colors. Pull from a reference image, a movie still, an album cover. Lock the palette before you grade — you'll know when to stop pushing because you'll have hit the target.</li>
  </StepsList>

  <Shot src={step1} alt="Photoshop Mode menu set to 16 Bits/Channel and Color Settings dialog with sRGB working space" />
  <Shot src={step2} alt="Reference image alongside a 3–5 color target palette" />

  <Why><strong>Decisions made before any layer goes down compound.</strong> Skip the palette and you'll spend the next hour wandering toward a look you can't articulate. The non-pass pass is where the rest of the work becomes <em>finishable</em>.</Why>

  <Mistakes items={[
    { symptomHtml: "Banding shows up in skies or smooth gradients halfway through the grade", fixHtml: "<strong>You're working in 8-bit.</strong> Image → Mode → 16 Bits/Channel before any adjustment layer goes down. The extra precision absorbs heavy stacks without breaking up." },
    { symptomHtml: "Forty minutes in and you can't decide what the image should look like", fixHtml: "<strong>You skipped the palette.</strong> Stop. Pull 3–5 colors from a reference (movie still, album cover, photo). Lock them. Resume — now you have a stop condition." }
  ]} />
</GuideStep>

<GuideStep id="pass-2" num="02" kicker="Tonal Anchor · ~5 min · Monochrome thinking" title="Fix the tonality first.">
  <Goal>Establish black point, white point, and overall contrast on the master RGB curve — before any individual channel work.</Goal>

  <StepsList>
    <li><strong>Levels for the anchors.</strong> Layer → New Adjustment Layer → <code>Levels</code>. Drag the black slider in until the histogram starts. Drag the white slider in until the histogram ends. You're snapping the tonal range to its actual edges.</li>
    <li><strong>Curves for contrast shape.</strong> Layer → New Adjustment Layer → <code>Curves</code>. Master RGB channel only. Apply a subtle <em>S-curve</em>: click once in the middle of the diagonal to drop an anchor, then drag the lower portion <em>down</em> a touch and the upper portion <em>up</em> a touch. Don't go heavy — you'll add more in Pass 03. <em>Optional refinement:</em> set the Curves layer's blend mode to <code>Luminosity</code> so the contrast move doesn't quietly pump saturation as a side effect. Skip the Luminosity move if you actually want that punch — decide deliberately, not by accident.</li>
    <li><strong>Sanity check.</strong> Hold <kbd>Alt</kbd>/<kbd>Option</kbd> and click the eyeball icon next to your source image layer (bottom-most in the Layers panel) — this temporarily hides all the adjustment layers above it, letting you A/B against the original. Image should feel <em>cleaner</em>, not <em>different</em>. If it already looks "graded," you went too far — back off.</li>
  </StepsList>

  <Shot src={step3} alt="Levels dialog with the histogram and black/white sliders pulled to the edges of the data" />
  <Shot src={step4} alt="Curves panel showing a subtle master-RGB S-curve" />

  <Why><strong>Color sits on top of contrast.</strong> Anchor the tonality first and Pass 03 paints onto a stable canvas; skip it and every Curves move in the color pass fights muddy midtones. Tonal anchor before color also stops you from chasing saturation that's actually a contrast problem.</Why>

  <Mistakes items={[
    { symptomHtml: "Image already looks \"graded\" after Levels and Curves", fixHtml: "<strong>You went too far.</strong> Pull the S-curve back toward neutral. This pass is meant to feel <em>cleaner</em>, not <em>different</em>." },
    { symptomHtml: "Skin looks washed out or oversaturated after the contrast move", fixHtml: "<strong>Set the Curves layer to <code>Luminosity</code> blend mode.</strong> You get the contrast without the saturation pump that S-curves quietly add." }
  ]} />
</GuideStep>

<GuideStep id="pass-3" num="03" kicker="The Color Pass · ~25 min · The meat" title="Where the look gets built.">
  <Goal>Build the color story through four stacked tools — Curves channels for structure, Selective Color for surgical hue shifts, Hue/Saturation for protection, Solid Color wash as a final unifier — without any single move doing all the work.</Goal>

  <SubPass num="3.1" titleHtml="Curves channels — <em>the split-tone skeleton</em>">
    <p>Layer → New Adjustment Layer → <code>Curves</code>. The structural backbone of every color grade. Each channel curve doesn't control brightness — it controls <strong>how much of one specific color lives in each tonal zone</strong>. Once this clicks, most grades become obvious.</p>
    <p><strong>The three channels each push along one color axis:</strong></p>
    <ul>
      <li><strong>Red curve</strong> — pushes between <em>Red ↔ Cyan</em></li>
      <li><strong>Green curve</strong> — pushes between <em>Green ↔ Magenta</em></li>
      <li><strong>Blue curve</strong> — pushes between <em>Blue ↔ Yellow</em></li>
    </ul>
    <p><strong>Reading any curve:</strong> bottom-left = shadows, middle = midtones, top-right = highlights. <em>Lifting</em> a point adds more of that channel's color in that zone; <em>dropping</em> a point shifts that zone toward the channel's opposite. So dropping the top-right of the Blue curve gives you warm (yellow) highlights. Lifting the bottom-left of the Red curve gives you red shadows. <strong>Pair moves across channels to build split-tones</strong> — warm one tonal end, cool the other.</p>
    <p>Keep moves small — <code>5–15 points</code>. The eye exaggerates color shifts faster than you'd expect, and it's easier to add than subtract.</p>
    <p><em>What the Lynae case study shows:</em> its magenta-shadow / cyan-highlight signature is mostly Red channel curve work, pushed slightly harder than usual — bottom-left lifted for the magenta shadows, top-right dropped for the cyan highlights. One channel doing most of the lifting.</p>
    <p><em>One note tying back to Pass 02:</em> this is one of the rare times you do <strong>not</strong> want Luminosity blend mode on your Curves layer. The whole point here is to shift color — Luminosity would strip exactly the change you're making. Default <code>Normal</code> blend mode is correct. (Advanced: <code>Color</code> blend mode locks <em>only</em> the color shift with no contrast bleed — useful when stacking many channel curves.)</p>
    <Shot src={step5} alt="Curves panel with a single channel curve adjusted — endpoints shifted to push that channel's color axis" />
    <Shot src={step6} alt="Curves panel with a second channel curve now active, showing the paired-channel split-tone setup" />
  </SubPass>

  <SubPass num="3.2" titleHtml="Selective Color — <em>surgical hue zones</em>">
    <p>Layer → New Adjustment Layer → <code>Selective Color</code>. The cleanest tool for adjusting one specific hue zone without affecting the others. Where Curves channels paint with a roller, Selective Color paints with a brush.</p>
    <p><strong>How it works:</strong></p>
    <ul>
      <li>The <strong>Colors dropdown</strong> picks which family you're adjusting — <em>Reds, Yellows, Greens, Cyans, Blues, Magentas</em> for hue ranges, plus <em>Whites, Neutrals, Blacks</em> which target tonal zones rather than hues.</li>
      <li>The <strong>four sliders</strong> (Cyan, Magenta, Yellow, Black) shift the selected family toward or away from those colors. <code>+Cyan</code> on a Red range = less red. <code>+Magenta</code> on a Red range = more pink. Same logic for every family.</li>
      <li><strong>Whites, Neutrals, Blacks</strong> are the most underused options. They let you tint a tonal zone (your white shirt, your shadow areas) without touching the saturated colors elsewhere in the image.</li>
    </ul>
    <p><strong>Mode toggle:</strong> <code>Relative</code> for subtle, proportional shifts (start here). <code>Absolute</code> for stronger, more obvious shifts (switch when a zone won't budge).</p>
    <p><strong>A few representative moves</strong> to show the logic — adapt the specifics to whatever target palette you committed to in Pass 01:</p>
    <ul>
      <li>Push reds toward orange — select Reds, <code>+Yellow</code></li>
      <li>Push reds toward pink — select Reds, <code>+Magenta</code></li>
      <li>Desaturate reds without shifting their hue — select Reds, <code>+Cyan</code> (red's opposite)</li>
      <li>Tint a white shirt cool — select Whites, <code>−Yellow</code> with a touch of <code>+Cyan</code></li>
      <li>Tint shadows warm — select Blacks, <code>−Cyan, +Yellow</code></li>
    </ul>
    <p>Once "each slider pushes toward or away from one color" clicks, you can construct any move you need.</p>
    <Shot src={step7} alt="Selective Color panel with one Colors range selected, the four sliders shifted to push it toward an adjacent hue" />
    <Shot src={step8} alt="Selective Color panel with a different Colors range selected, showing the same slider logic applied to another family" />
  </SubPass>

  <SubPass num="3.3" titleHtml="Hue/Saturation — <em>the protection pass</em>">
    <p>New adjustment layer → <code>Hue/Saturation</code>. The protective pass, mostly. After the color decisions in 3.1 and 3.2, certain zones will end up too punchy or shifted out of place — most commonly, skin tones get dragged into the dominant palette and stop reading as skin. Hue/Sat with the eyedropper is your scalpel for fixing that.</p>
    <p><strong>Vibrance vs Hue/Sat — the critical distinction:</strong></p>
    <ul>
      <li><strong>Vibrance</strong> — the smart global slider. Protects skin tones automatically; boosts undersaturated colors more than already-saturated ones. Reach for it when you want overall punch.</li>
      <li><strong>Hue/Sat with a specific range</strong> — surgical. Reach for it when you want targeted control. The <em>master</em> Saturation slider on Hue/Sat is rarely the right tool; the <em>range dropdown</em> is where the precision lives.</li>
    </ul>
    <p><strong>Workflow:</strong> Add a Hue/Saturation adjustment layer → pick the range that's misbehaving from the dropdown (Reds, Yellows, etc.), <em>or</em> use the eyedropper to click directly on the offending color in the image, which sets the range automatically → pull Saturation down to taste (<code>−10 to −30</code> is typical for skin protection; further if a color is really screaming) → optionally nudge Hue <code>±5</code> to fine-tune the actual color of that range.</p>
    <p>The <strong>eyedropper plus the range bar at the bottom of the panel</strong> are the underused features. The range bar lets you widen or narrow exactly which hues your adjustment affects — drag the inner stops to set the targeted range, drag the outer stops to set the falloff.</p>
    <Shot src={step9} alt="Hue/Saturation panel with the eyedropper engaged and the bottom range bar visible, showing inner/outer falloff stops" />
  </SubPass>

  <SubPass num="3.4" titleHtml="Bonus — <em>Solid Color wash</em>">
    <p>Layer → New Fill Layer → <code>Solid Color</code>. Set blend mode to <code>Color</code> at 5–15% opacity, or <code>Soft Light</code> at 10–20% for a gentle warming/cooling. Use as a stitch when everything looks right but the image feels visually disconnected.</p>
    <Shot src={step10} alt="Solid Color fill layer at the top of the stack with low opacity, blend mode Color" />
  </SubPass>

  <Why><strong>Color builds in passes.</strong> Curves channels paint with a roller; Selective Color paints with a brush; Hue/Sat protects what shouldn't move. Stacked, they read as a grade. One global slam — even a "vibrant action" preset — reads as a filter. The principle that runs through all four sub-passes: <strong>vibrant ≠ saturated.</strong></Why>

  <Mistakes items={[
    { symptomHtml: "Whole-image color cast — skin, whites, shadows all tinted the same dominant hue", fixHtml: "<strong>One channel curve pushed too hard with no Hue/Sat protection on neutrals.</strong> Pull back the dominant Curves move or add a stronger Hue/Sat protection layer on the affected ranges." },
    { symptomHtml: "Crushed shadow detail — detail drops out of dark zones; skin in low light takes on the lifted hue (corpse-blue, sickly green, etc.)", fixHtml: "<strong>Channel curve bottom-left lifted past 20+ points (or master Curves shadow lifted hard).</strong> Back off the lift." },
    { symptomHtml: "\"Instagram filter\" look", fixHtml: "<strong>One strong global shift instead of layered passes.</strong> Diagnostic: if toggling off any single layer kills the whole grade, you're relying on one big move. Spread it across 3–4 smaller layers." },
    { symptomHtml: "Reaching for master saturation when Vibrance was the right tool", fixHtml: "<strong>Master saturation in Hue/Saturation is rarely correct.</strong> Global punch → Vibrance. Targeted punch → Hue/Sat with a specific range. Never the master." }
  ]} />
</GuideStep>

<GuideStep id="pass-4" num="04" kicker="Atmosphere & Light · ~10 min" title="Make it feel lit, not graded.">
  <Goal>Add directional color light cues — rim, bloom, leaks — that make the palette feel emitted from the scene rather than overlaid on it.</Goal>

  <StepsList>
    <li><strong>Rim lighting, matched to background.</strong> New layer above subject, blend mode <code>Screen</code> or <code>Linear Dodge</code>. Soft brush in whichever color sits <em>behind</em> that edge of the subject. Paint a thin line along the subject's edge facing that color source. If different colors sit behind different sides of the subject, repeat with a second (or third) rim on each respective side. The rule isn't "use complementary colors" — it's <em>"match what's actually back there"</em>. Whatever the environment is doing, the rim should echo.</li>
    <li><strong>Bloom on highlights.</strong> Stamp visible (<kbd>Ctrl/Cmd + Shift + Alt + E</kbd>) → Filter → Blur → <code>Gaussian Blur 30–50px</code> → set blend to <code>Lighten</code> or <code>Screen</code> at 20–40% opacity. <em>Stamp visible just makes a single flattened copy of everything you see, on a new layer above — your originals stay untouched.</em> The image's brightest points now glow.</li>
    <li><strong>Light leaks (optional).</strong> Drop a colored gradient layer or a leak overlay PNG, set to <code>Screen</code> at 40–70%. <em>Pick a color that already exists in your palette</em> — leaks that introduce a new hue read as "filter," leaks that amplify an existing one read as "light." Position matters too: leaks at corners and edges feel diegetic; leaks across the subject's face feel imposed.</li>
    <li><strong>Sparkle &amp; accent particles (optional).</strong> Tiny soft brush dots on a Screen layer, in a light color from your palette. The principle is restraint — a handful of placed accents reads as intentional; a dense field of them reads as overlay. Exact density is style-dependent. Place them where the eye should travel, not uniformly.</li>
  </StepsList>

  <Shot src={step11} alt="Two rim-light layers, one each side of the subject, blend mode Screen, in complementary colors" />
  <Shot src={step12} alt="Stamped + Gaussian-blurred bloom layer above the comp at Screen ~40% opacity" />
  <Shot src={step13} alt="Sparkle accents on a Screen layer, soft round brush at small size" />

  <Why><strong>A graded image and a lit image read very differently.</strong> Pass 03 sets the palette; this pass makes it feel like the colors are <em>coming from somewhere</em>. Rim, bloom, and sparkle are the difference between a colored image and a dreamy one.</Why>

  <Mistakes items={[
    { symptomHtml: "Rim lights look like glowing outlines", fixHtml: "<strong>Drop the layer opacity</strong> to 30–50% and soften the brush. Rim should be <em>almost</em> imperceptible — glowing means wrong." },
    { symptomHtml: "Sparkle accents read as dirty pixels or noise", fixHtml: "<strong>Too many.</strong> Cap at 4–8 across the whole image and place them on existing highlights, not in dark zones." }
  ]} />
</GuideStep>

<GuideStep id="pass-5" num="05" kicker="Finishing · ~5 min · Out the door" title="The last 5% that ships.">
  <Goal>Apply a final unifying pass via Camera Raw, add grain on the merged image (not per-layer), and export with banding-safe settings.</Goal>

  <StepsList>
    <li><strong>Camera Raw as smart filter.</strong> Stamp visible → right-click → <code>Convert to Smart Object</code> → Filter → <code>Camera Raw Filter</code>. Use the Color Grading wheels (shadows / midtones / highlights) for one final unifying pass. <em>Because it's applied to a Smart Object, the Camera Raw settings stay editable — double-click the filter name in the Layers panel to reopen and tweak.</em></li>
    <li><strong>Vignette.</strong> Inside Camera Raw → Effects → Post-Crop Vignetting. Amount <code>−15 to −25</code>. Or skip Camera Raw and use a black radial gradient layer at <code>Multiply 30–50%</code>.</li>
    <li><strong>Grain on the merged comp.</strong> Camera Raw → Effects → Grain. Amount <code>15–25</code>, Size <code>15–25</code>, Roughness <code>50</code>. Critical: grain on the <em>final composite</em>, not the layers individually. Unifies the whole image.</li>
    <li><strong>Export discipline.</strong> File → Export → Export As. Format <code>JPG</code>, Quality <code>80–90</code>, Color space <code>sRGB</code>, <strong>Convert to sRGB checked</strong>. The detail readers miss most.</li>
  </StepsList>

  <Shot src={step14} alt="Camera Raw Filter Color Grading panel with shadow/midtone/highlight wheels positioned" />
  <Shot src={step15} alt="Camera Raw Effects Grain settings dialed in (Amount, Size, Roughness)" />
  <Shot src={step16} alt="Photoshop Export As dialog with JPG, Quality 80–90, and Convert to sRGB ticked" />

  <Why><strong>Grain on the merged comp is what ties everything together.</strong> Per-layer grain reads as noise; one grain pass on the final composite makes the eye stop seeing layers and start seeing one image. The Camera Raw color-grading wheels do the same thing for color — one final unifier across the whole stack.</Why>

  <Mistakes items={[
    { symptomHtml: "Banding showed up in the export — visible stripes in skies or smooth gradients", fixHtml: "<strong>Add a micro-noise layer before flattening.</strong> New Layer → fill 50% gray → blend mode <code>Soft Light</code> → Filter → Noise → Add Noise at 1–2%. The noise breaks up the banded edges and disappears into the grain you added in step 3." },
    { symptomHtml: "Grain looks chunky or pixelated on phones", fixHtml: "<strong>Drop Size to ~15</strong> and bump Amount slightly. Big grain reads as digital noise; fine grain reads as film." }
  ]} />
</GuideStep>
